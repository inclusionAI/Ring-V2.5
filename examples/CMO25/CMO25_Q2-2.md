**Step 1. Inversion and the position of $K$.**  

Let $d=OP$. The circumcircle has centre $O$ and radius $1$. Consider the inversion $\mathcal I$ with centre $P$ and radius $r$ that leaves the circumcircle invariant. For orthogonality we need  
$$
OP^2 = r^2 + 1^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad r^2 = d^2-1. \tag{1.1}
$$

Under $\mathcal I$:  
- The line $BA$ (through $P$) is invariant, and $B$ maps to $A$.  
- The line $CD$ (through $P$) is invariant, and $C$ maps to $D$.  
- The line $BC$ (not through $P$) maps to the circle $\Gamma$ through $P,A,D$.

The hypothesis gives $K\in BC$ and $P,A,K,D$ concyclic, i.e. $K\in\Gamma$.  

Because $K\in BC$, its image $K'=\mathcal I(K)$ lies on $\Gamma$. Also $K$ lies on line $PK$, and any line through the centre of inversion is mapped to itself, so $K'$ also lies on line $PK$. The line $PK$ meets $\Gamma$ at $P$ and at $K$ (since $K\in\Gamma$ and $K\neq P$). The point $P$ is the centre of inversion, so its image is the point at infinity; therefore $K'\neq P$. Consequently $K'=K$, i.e. $K$ is fixed by $\mathcal I$. Hence  
$$
PK = r = \sqrt{d^2-1}. \tag{1.2}
$$

The power of $K$ with respect to the circumcircle is $KB\cdot KC = 1 - OK^2$ (since $K$ is inside the unit circle). The problem gives  
$$
KB\cdot KC = \frac12 d^2. \tag{1.3}
$$
Thus  
$$
1 - OK^2 = \frac12 d^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad OK^2 = 1 - \frac{d^2}{2}. \tag{1.4}
$$

Now consider $\triangle OKP$. By the triangle inequality,  
$$
OK + PK \ge OP = d.
$$
Substituting (1.2) and (1.4) and writing $x=d^2$ yields, after squaring and simplifying,  
$$
(3x-4)^2 \le 0.
$$
Hence equality holds, so $x=\frac43$ and  
$$
d = OP = \frac{2}{\sqrt3}. \tag{1.5}
$$
Moreover, the triangle inequality becomes an equality, which implies that $O,K,P$ are collinear with $K$ between $O$ and $P$. Then from (1.2) and (1.4),  
$$
PK = OK = \sqrt{\frac43-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt3} =: a. \tag{1.6}
$$

Thus $K$ is the midpoint of $OP$. Set $a=\frac1{\sqrt3}$.

---

**Step 2. Coordinate system and the chord $BC$.**  

Place $K$ at the origin, let the $x$-axis be the line $OP$ directed from $O$ to $P$. Then  
$$
O=(-a,0),\qquad P=(a,0).
$$

Let the line $BC$ make an angle $\theta$ with the positive $x$-axis. Points on $BC$ are $t(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$. Substituting into the equation of the circumcircle $(x+a)^2+y^2=1$ gives  
$$
(t\cos\theta+a)^2 + (t\sin\theta)^2 = t^2 + 2a\cos\theta\,t + a^2 = 1.
$$
Using $a^2=\frac13$ we obtain the quadratic  
$$
t^2 + 2a\cos\theta\,t - \frac23 = 0. \tag{2.1}
$$
Its roots are $\lambda$ and $\mu$ with $\lambda>0$, $\mu<0$ (product negative). Then  
$$
B = (\lambda\cos\theta,\;\lambda\sin\theta),\qquad 
C = (\mu\cos\theta,\;\mu\sin\theta).
$$
From (2.1) we have the fundamental relations  
$$
\lambda+\mu = -2a\cos\theta,\qquad \lambda\mu = -\frac23. \tag{2.2}
$$
For later use we also set  
$$
c = a\cos\theta, \qquad d = \sqrt{c^2 + \frac23}\; (>0),
$$
so that $\lambda = -c + d,\;\; \mu = -c - d$.

---

**Step 3. Points $A$ and $D$ via inversion.**  

The inversion $\mathcal I$ from Step 1 (centre $P$, radius $a$) sends $B$ to $A$ and $C$ to $D$. For any point $X\neq P$,  
$$
\mathcal I(X) = P + \frac{a^2}{|X-P|^2}\,(X-P).
$$
Compute  
$$
|B-P|^2 = (\lambda\cos\theta-a)^2 + (\lambda\sin\theta)^2 = \lambda^2 - 2a\lambda\cos\theta + a^2.
$$
Using $\lambda^2 = -2a\cos\theta\,\lambda + \frac23$ (from (2.1)) and $a^2=\frac13$, we get  
$$
|B-P|^2 = \Bigl(-2a\cos\theta\,\lambda + \frac23\Bigr) - 2a\lambda\cos\theta + a^2
= \frac23 + a^2 - 4a\lambda\cos\theta = 1 - 4a\lambda\cos\theta.
$$
Set  
$$
U = 1 - 4a\lambda\cos\theta,\qquad V = 1 - 4a\mu\cos\theta.
$$
Define  
$$
\alpha = \frac{a^2}{U},\qquad \beta = \frac{a^2}{V}.
$$
Then  
$$
A = (1-\alpha)P + \alpha B,\qquad D = (1-\beta)P + \beta C. \tag{3.1}
$$

---

**Step 4. Barycentric coordinates of $Q = AC\cap BD$.**  

Work in barycentric coordinates with respect to $\triangle PBC$. Assign  
$$
P = (1,0,0),\quad B = (0,1,0),\quad C = (0,0,1).
$$
From (3.1) we have  
$$
A = (1-\alpha,\;\alpha,\;0),\qquad D = (1-\beta,\;0,\;\beta).
$$
Parametrise line $AC$ as $A + s(C-A)$. This gives points of the form  
$$
\bigl((1-\alpha)(1-s),\; \alpha(1-s),\; s\bigr). \tag{4.1}
$$
Parametrise line $BD$ as $B + t(D-B)$, giving  
$$
\bigl(t(1-\beta),\; 1-t,\; t\beta\bigr). \tag{4.2}
$$
Equating (4.1) and (4.2) yields the system  
$$
\begin{cases}
(1-\alpha)(1-s) = t(1-\beta),\\
\alpha(1-s) = 1-t,\\
s = t\beta.
\end{cases}
$$
Solving (the details are straightforward) we obtain  
$$
s = \frac{\beta(1-\alpha)}{1-\alpha\beta},\qquad
t = \frac{1-\alpha}{1-\alpha\beta},
$$
and consequently  
$$
Q = \left(\frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\beta)}{1-\alpha\beta},\;
          \frac{\alpha(1-\beta)}{1-\alpha\beta},\;
          \frac{\beta(1-\alpha)}{1-\alpha\beta}\right). \tag{4.3}
$$

---

**Step 5. Projection of $Q$ onto line $BC$.**  

Let $\mathbf{d}=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$ be a unit vector along $BC$. For any point $X$ with barycentric coordinates $(x_P,x_B,x_C)$ its position vector (with origin at $K$) is  
$$
\vec{X} = x_P\,\vec{P} + x_B\,\vec{B} + x_C\,\vec{C}.
$$
The signed distance along $BC$ from $K$ (the $u$-coordinate) is $u_X = \vec{X}\cdot\mathbf{d}$.  
We compute  
$$
\vec{P}\cdot\mathbf{d} = (a,0)\cdot(\cos\theta,\sin\theta) = a\cos\theta = c,
$$
$$
\vec{B}\cdot\mathbf{d} = \lambda(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) = \lambda,
$$
$$
\vec{C}\cdot\mathbf{d} = \mu.
$$
Hence, using (4.3),  
$$
u_Q = \frac{ (1-\alpha)(1-\beta)\,c \;+\; \alpha(1-\beta)\,\lambda \;+\; \beta(1-\alpha)\,\mu }{1-\alpha\beta}. \tag{5.1}
$$
Substitute $\alpha = a^2/U$, $\beta = a^2/V$ and multiply numerator and denominator by $UV$:  
$$
u_Q = \frac{ (U-a^2)(V-a^2)\,c \;+\; a^2(V-a^2)\,\lambda \;+\; a^2(U-a^2)\,\mu }{UV - a^4}. \tag{5.2}
$$

Now we express everything in terms of $c = a\cos\theta$. From the definitions,  
$$
U = 1 - 4c\lambda,\qquad V = 1 - 4c\mu,
$$
and from (2.2) we have $\lambda+\mu = -2c$, $\lambda\mu = -\frac23$. Also recall $a^2 = \frac13$.  

Compute the ingredients step by step:

$$
\begin{aligned}
U - a^2 &= (1 - a^2) - 4c\lambda = \frac23 - 4c\lambda,\\
V - a^2 &= \frac23 - 4c\mu.
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
(U-a^2)(V-a^2) &= \Bigl(\frac23 - 4c\lambda\Bigr)\Bigl(\frac23 - 4c\mu\Bigr)\\
&= \frac49 - \frac{8c}{3}(\lambda+\mu) + 16c^2\lambda\mu\\
&= \frac49 - \frac{8c}{3}(-2c) + 16c^2\Bigl(-\frac23\Bigr)\\
&= \frac49 + \frac{16c^2}{3} - \frac{32c^2}{3} = \frac49 - \frac{16c^2}{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
a^2(V-a^2)\lambda + a^2(U-a^2)\mu 
&= a^2\Bigl[ \lambda\Bigl(\frac23 - 4c\mu\Bigr) + \mu\Bigl(\frac23 - 4c\lambda\Bigr) \Bigr]\\
&= a^2\Bigl[ \frac23(\lambda+\mu) - 8c\lambda\mu \Bigr]\\
&= a^2\Bigl[ \frac23(-2c) - 8c\Bigl(-\frac23\Bigr) \Bigr]\\
&= a^2\Bigl( -\frac{4c}{3} + \frac{16c}{3} \Bigr) = a^2 \cdot 4c = 4a^2 c.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $a^2 = \frac13$, the numerator $N$ of (5.2) becomes  

$$
\begin{aligned}
N &= c\Bigl(\frac49 - \frac{16c^2}{3}\Bigr) + 4a^2 c 
   = \frac{4c}{9} - \frac{16c^3}{3} + \frac{4c}{3}\\
   &= \frac{4c}{9} + \frac{12c}{9} - \frac{48c^3}{9}
   = \frac{16c}{9} - \frac{48c^3}{9}
   = \frac{16c(1-3c^2)}{9}.
\end{aligned}
$$

For the denominator $D = UV - a^4$ we have  

$$
\begin{aligned}
UV &= (1 - 4c\lambda)(1 - 4c\mu) 
    = 1 - 4c(\lambda+\mu) + 16c^2\lambda\mu\\
   &= 1 - 4c(-2c) + 16c^2\Bigl(-\frac23\Bigr)
    = 1 + 8c^2 - \frac{32c^2}{3}
    = 1 - \frac{8c^2}{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus  

$$
D = \Bigl(1 - \frac{8c^2}{3}\Bigr) - \frac{1}{9}
   = \frac{9}{9} - \frac{24c^2}{9} - \frac{1}{9}
   = \frac{8 - 24c^2}{9}
   = \frac{8(1-3c^2)}{9}.
$$

Note that $1-3c^2 = 1 - 3a^2\cos^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta = \sin^2\theta > 0$ (since $\theta$ is not a multiple of $\pi$ in a non‑degenerate configuration). Therefore  

$$
u_Q = \frac{N}{D} = \frac{ \dfrac{16c(1-3c^2)}{9} }{ \dfrac{8(1-3c^2)}{9} } = 2c = 2a\cos\theta. \tag{5.3}
$$

---

**Step 6. Location of $T$.**  

The line through $Q$ perpendicular to $BC$ is, in a coordinate system where the $u$-axis is aligned with $BC$, the vertical line $u = u_Q = 2a\cos\theta$.  

Line $OP$ is the $x$-axis. In the rotated frame, a point on $OP$ has coordinates $(x\cos\theta,\,-x\sin\theta)$. Its $u$-coordinate equals $x\cos\theta$. Setting this equal to $2a\cos\theta$ gives $x\cos\theta = 2a\cos\theta$. Since the geometry forces $\cos\theta \neq 0$ (as will be seen from the angle conditions), we obtain $x = 2a$. Hence the intersection point is  

$$
T = (2a,\,0) \quad \text{(in the original coordinate system with origin at }K\text{)}. \tag{6.1}
$$

---

**Step 7. Computation of $TB+TC$.**  

Using the coordinates of $B$, $C$, and $T$ we compute the squared distances:

$$
\begin{aligned}
TB^2 &= (\lambda\cos\theta - 2a)^2 + (\lambda\sin\theta)^2 
     = \lambda^2 - 4a\lambda\cos\theta + 4a^2,\\
TC^2 &= \mu^2 - 4a\mu\cos\theta + 4a^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

From (2.1) we have $\lambda^2 = -2a\cos\theta\,\lambda + \frac23$ and similarly for $\mu^2$. Substituting,

$$
TB^2 = \Bigl(-2a\cos\theta\,\lambda + \frac23\Bigr) - 4a\lambda\cos\theta + 4a^2
      = \frac23 + 4a^2 - 6a\lambda\cos\theta.
$$

Since $4a^2 + \frac23 = \frac43 + \frac23 = 2$, we get  

$$
TB^2 = 2 - 6a\lambda\cos\theta,\qquad 
TC^2 = 2 - 6a\mu\cos\theta. \tag{7.1}
$$

Now  

$$
\begin{aligned}
TB^2 + TC^2 &= 4 - 6a\cos\theta\,(\lambda+\mu)
            = 4 - 6a\cos\theta\,(-2a\cos\theta)
            = 4 + 12a^2\cos^2\theta\\
            &= 4 + 12\cdot\frac13\cos^2\theta
            = 4 + 4\cos^2\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
TB^2\cdot TC^2 &= (2 - 6a\lambda\cos\theta)(2 - 6a\mu\cos\theta)\\
               &= 4 - 12a\cos\theta(\lambda+\mu) + 36a^2\cos^2\theta\,\lambda\mu\\
               &= 4 - 12a\cos\theta(-2a\cos\theta) + 36a^2\cos^2\theta\Bigl(-\frac23\Bigr)\\
               &= 4 + 24a^2\cos^2\theta - 24a^2\cos^2\theta = 4.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore  

$$
(TB+TC)^2 = (TB^2+TC^2) + 2\sqrt{TB^2\cdot TC^2} = (4+4\cos^2\theta) + 2\cdot 2 = 8 + 4\cos^2\theta,
$$
and  

$$
TB+TC = 2\sqrt{2 + \cos^2\theta}\, \qquad (\text{the positive root}). \tag{7.2}
$$

---

**Step 8. Restrictions on $\theta$ from the hypotheses.**  

We now translate the conditions $\angle ABC < \angle BCD < 90^\circ$ and the convexity of $ABCD$ into inequalities for $\theta$.

First compute $\cos\angle ABC$ and $\cos\angle BCD$.  
From the expressions for the vectors (or by direct calculation using the coordinates) one obtains  

$$
\cos\angle ABC = \frac{\lambda - a\cos\theta}{\sqrt{U}},\qquad 
\cos\angle BCD = \frac{a\cos\theta - \mu}{\sqrt{V}},
$$
where $U = 1 - 4a\lambda\cos\theta,\; V = 1 - 4a\mu\cos\theta$.  
(Verification: using $\overrightarrow{BA} = (1-\alpha)(P-B)$ and $\overrightarrow{BC}=(\mu-\lambda)(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$ gives the formula after simplification.)

Because both angles are acute, we require the numerators positive:

$$
\lambda > a\cos\theta,\qquad a\cos\theta > \mu. \tag{8.1}
$$

Set $c = a\cos\theta$. With $\lambda = -c+d,\;\mu = -c-d$ where $d = \sqrt{c^2 + \frac23}$, conditions (8.1) become  

$$
-c+d > c \;\Longrightarrow\; d > 2c,\qquad c > -c-d \;\Longrightarrow\; d > -2c.
$$
Together they are equivalent to $d > 2|c|$. Squaring gives  

$$
c^2 + \frac23 > 4c^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac23 > 3c^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad c^2 < \frac{2}{9}.
$$
In terms of $\theta$, since $a = 1/\!\sqrt3$, $c = \cos\theta/\!\sqrt3$, this inequality becomes  

$$
\frac{\cos^2\theta}{3} < \frac{2}{9} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \cos^2\theta < \frac{2}{3}. \tag{8.2}
$$

Next, the condition $\angle ABC < \angle BCD$. Because both angles are acute, this is equivalent to $\cos\angle ABC > \cos\angle BCD$. Substituting the expressions and simplifying (as in the algebra leading to (5.3)) yields  

$$
c\,(c^2 - a^2) > 0. \tag{8.3}
$$

From (8.2) we have $c^2 < \frac29 < \frac13 = a^2$, so $c^2 - a^2 < 0$. Therefore (8.3) forces  

$$
c < 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \cos\theta < 0. \tag{8.4}
$$

Finally, convexity of $ABCD$ demands that $A$ and $D$ lie on the same side of line $BC$.  
Take the unit normal to $BC$ given by $\mathbf{n} = (-\sin\theta,\cos\theta)$. Using (3.1) we find  

$$
A\cdot\mathbf{n} = (1-\alpha)(P\cdot\mathbf{n}) + \alpha(B\cdot\mathbf{n}) = (1-\alpha)(-a\sin\theta),
$$
because $B\cdot\mathbf{n}=0$ (B lies on BC). Similarly $D\cdot\mathbf{n} = (1-\beta)(-a\sin\theta)$.  
For $A$ and $D$ to be on the same side of $BC$ we need $(1-\alpha)$ and $(1-\beta)$ to have the same sign. Since $\alpha,\beta > 0$ and the geometry suggests both $A$ and $D$ are inside the circle (hence below $BC$ if we take $\sin\theta>0$), we require $1-\alpha > 0$ and $1-\beta > 0$, i.e. $\alpha < 1,\;\beta < 1$.  
Because $\alpha = a^2/U$ and $\beta = a^2/V$, this is equivalent to $U > a^2$ and $V > a^2$.  

One checks that under (8.2) and (8.4) we automatically have $U > a^2$ (since $U = 1 + 4c^2 - 4c d > 1 > a^2$). The condition $V > a^2$ is non‑trivial. With $c = -x$ ($x>0$), $V = 1 + 4x^2 - 4x\sqrt{x^2+\frac23}$. The inequality $V > \frac13$ becomes  

$$
\frac23 + 4x^2 > 4x\sqrt{x^2+\tfrac23}.
$$
Squaring (both sides are positive) and simplifying gives  

$$
x < \frac{1}{2\sqrt3} = \frac{a}{2}.
$$
Thus $c > -\frac{a}{2}$, i.e. $a\cos\theta > -\frac{a}{2}$ $\Longrightarrow$ $\cos\theta > -\frac12$. \tag{8.5}

Collecting (8.2), (8.4) and (8.5), and noting that (8.2) is automatically satisfied when $\cos\theta\in(-\frac12,0)$ (because $\frac14 < \frac23$), we obtain the admissible range  

$$
-\frac12 < \cos\theta < 0. \tag{8.6}
$$

The endpoints are excluded: $\cos\theta = 0$ would give $\angle ABC = \angle BCD$ (contradicting the strict inequality), and $\cos\theta = -\frac12$ makes $V = a^2$ so that $\beta = 1$, forcing $D = C$ and degeneracy.

---

**Step 9. Range of $TB+TC$.**  

From (7.2) we have $TB+TC = 2\sqrt{2+\cos^2\theta}$. As $\cos\theta$ varies continuously in the open interval $(-1/2,0)$, $\cos^2\theta$ takes all values in $(0,1/4)$. Consequently,  

$$
\lim_{\cos\theta\to 0^-} TB+TC = 2\sqrt{2},\qquad
\lim_{\cos\theta\to (-1/2)^+} TB+TC = 2\sqrt{2+\tfrac14} = 3,
$$
and every value between $2\sqrt2$ and $3$ is attained. Because the endpoints are not included, the set of possible values of $TB+TC$ is the open interval  

$$
(2\sqrt{2},\;3).
$$

---

**10. Conclusion**  

We have shown that under the given conditions the quantity $TB+TC$ can take any value in $(2\sqrt2,\,3)$, and no other values are possible.  

$$
\boxed{(2\sqrt{2},\;3)}.
$$
